Theory - Having an excess on your policy will help to keep the premium down. However, if you make a claim, you’ll have to pay the excess yourself – in this case, £100.
Question - You claim on your insurance to have your car repaired. Your policy has an excess of £100. What does this mean?
Answer - You'll have to pay the first £100 of the cost of repairs to your car.

Theory - After passing your practical driving test, you can take further training. One option is known as the Pass Plus scheme. It’s designed to improve your basic driving skills and involves a series of modules, including night-time and motorway driving.
Question - What’s the purpose of the Pass Plus scheme?
Answer - To improve your basic driving skills.

Theory - Third-party insurance doesn’t cover damage to your own vehicle or injury to yourself. If you have a crash and your vehicle is damaged, you might have to carry out the repairs at your own expense.
Question - You have third-party insurance. What does this cover?
Answer - Damage to other vehicles.

Theory - The registered keeper of the vehicle is responsible for paying the vehicle tax or making a Statutory Off-Road Notification (SORN) if the vehicle is to be kept untaxed and off the road.
Question - Who’s responsible for paying the vehicle tax?
Answer - The registered keeper of the vehicle.

Question - What information is found on a vehicle registration document?
Answer - The registered keeper.

Theory - Learner drivers benefit by combining professional driving lessons with private practice. However, you need to be at least 21 years old and have held your driving licence for at least 3 years before you can supervise a learner driver.
Question - Which of these is a requirement before you can supervise a learner driver?
Answer - You must be at least 21 years old.

Theory - Driving without insurance is a serious offence. As well as an unlimited fine, you may be disqualified or incur penalty points.
Question - What’s the maximum fine for driving without insurance?
Answer - Unlimited.

Theory - When a car is three years old (four years old in Northern Ireland), it must pass an MOT test and have a valid MOT certificate before it can be used on the road. Exceptionally, you may. drive to a pre-arranged test appointment or to a garage for repairs
Question - Your car requires an MOT certificate. When is it legal to drive it without an MOT certificate?
Answer - When driving to an appointment at an MOT centre.

Theory - It’s your legal responsibility to keep the details on your vehicle registration certificate (V5C) up to date. You should tell the licensing authority about any changes. These include your name, address or vehicle details. 
Question - Who’s legally responsible for ensuring that a vehicle registration certificate (V5C) is updated?
Answer - The registered vehicle keeper.

Theory - The vehicle you drive must be roadworthy and in good condition. If it’s over three years old, it must pass an MOT test to remain in use on the road (unless it’s exempt from the MOT test – see GOV.UK).
Question - A new car will need its first MOT test when it’s how old?
Answer - Three years.

Theory - You must produce a valid insurance certificate when requested by a police officer. If you can’t do this immediately, you may be asked to take it to a police station.
Question - In which of these circumstances must you show your insurance certificate?
Answer - When a police officer asks you for it.

Theory - New drivers are far more vulnerable on the road and more likely to be involved in incidents. The Pass Plus scheme has been designed to improve new drivers’ basic skills and help widen their driving experience.
Question - The Pass Plus scheme has been created for new drivers. What’s its main purpose?
Answer - To improve your basic skills.

Theory - Using a vehicle on the road illegally carries a heavy fine and can lead to penalty points on your driving licence. You must have
    • a valid driving licence
    • paid the appropriate vehicle tax
    • proper insurance cover.
Question - Which of these is needed before you can legally use a vehicle on the road?
Answer - A valid driving licence.

Theory - Third-party insurance cover is usually cheaper than comprehensive cover. However, it doesn’t cover any damage caused to your own vehicle or property. It only covers damage and injury you cause to others.
Question - Your vehicle is insured third-party only. What does this cover?
Answer - Damage to other vehicles.

Theory - You can renew your vehicle tax online, at post offices and vehicle registration offices, or by phone. When applying, make sure you have all the relevant valid documents, including a valid MOT test certificate where applicable.
Question - What must you have when you apply to renew your vehicle tax?
Answer - Valid insurance.

Theory - You don’t have to carry around your vehicle’s documents wherever you go. If a police officer asks to see them and you don’t have them with you, you may be asked to produce them at a police station within 7 days.
Question - A police officer asks to see your documents. You don’t have them with you. Within what time must you produce them at a police station?
Answer - 7 days.

Answer - Have valid motor insurance.

Theory - A SORN allows you to keep a vehicle off-road and untaxed. SORN will end when the vehicle is taxed, sold or scrapped.

Theory - If you want to keep a vehicle untaxed and off the public road, you must make a SORN. It’s an offence not to do so. Your SORN is valid until your vehicle is taxed, sold or scrapped.
Question - What is a Statutory Off-Road Notification (SORN)?
Answer - A notification to tell DVLA that a vehicle isn't being used on the road.
